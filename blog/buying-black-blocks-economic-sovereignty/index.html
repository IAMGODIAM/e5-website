Buying Black Blocks: The Blueprint for Economic Sovereignty in Liberty City

Cooperative ownership, Web3 tools, and strategic land acquisition as weapons against gentrification

ILA
Israel Lee Armstead
President & Chief Visionary
March 1, 2026 · 12 min read
economic sovereigntyliberty cityland ownershipcooperativeweb3
L

iberty City is changing. Drive down NW 62nd Street and you'll see it — new construction mixed with boarded-up buildings, coffee shops that didn't exist two years ago next to barbershops that have been there for forty. The pattern is familiar to anyone who has watched a Black neighborhood transform: capital arrives, land values rise, longtime residents leave. They call it progress. We call it displacement.

E5 Enclave's economic pillar exists because we refuse to accept the premise that gentrification is inevitable. It is not a natural force — it is a market outcome that can be countered with market tools. The question is not whether Black communities can compete in real estate markets. The question is whether they have the organizational structures to do so collectively.

The Buying Black Blocks Model

Our approach is straightforward: identify properties in Liberty City that are at risk of speculative acquisition, organize community capital to purchase them cooperatively, and structure ownership so that the land remains in community hands permanently. This is not theory. Community Land Trusts have been doing this across the country for decades. What we add is scale, technology, and urgency.

Every block we buy is a block they can't gentrify. Every deed in Black hands is a future secured.

The Web3 component is not decoration. Fractional ownership tokens allow community members to invest as little as $50 in a property acquisition. Smart contracts enforce the rules of the cooperative — no single member can sell their share to an outside developer without community consent. The blockchain provides the transparency and immutability that traditional cooperative structures often lack.

We are building the infrastructure for collective economic action. Every dollar pooled, every property secured, every deed transferred into cooperative ownership is a permanent victory against displacement.

The Legal Architecture

Enthusiasm without legal structure is just a rally. E5 Enclave operates with legal counsel at every step. Our cooperative model uses a hybrid LLC/Trust structure that provides: liability protection for individual members, tax-advantaged charitable contributions where applicable, deed restrictions that prevent speculative resale, and governance mechanisms that ensure democratic control.

We don't romanticize poverty. We don't pretend that economic sovereignty can be achieved through willpower alone. It requires capital, legal expertise, and organizational discipline. The 501(c)(3) structure of E5 Enclave allows us to receive tax-deductible donations that directly fund property acquisitions for community use.

Cooperative ownership isn't a utopian dream — it's a legal structure. And legal structures win.

Building Black Blocks

Buying is step one. Building is step two. Every property we acquire enters a development pipeline: assessment, community input, design, construction, and activation. A vacant lot might become a community garden (FarmBlock), a youth center, a co-working space for Black entrepreneurs, or affordable housing — the community decides.

This is the E5 model in practice: Environmental infrastructure on acquired land. Economic sovereignty through collective ownership. Educational programs housed in community-owned spaces. Equality through equitable access to resources. Emancipation through permanent self-determination.

The five pillars are not abstract values. They are an operational framework. And every block we buy, every building we raise, every program we house is a concrete expression of that framework.

The Numbers

In Liberty City, the median home price has increased 47% in the past five years. Commercial property values have nearly doubled in some corridors. For every month we delay, acquisition costs increase. This is not a slow-burn initiative — it is a race against speculative capital, and we intend to win it.

Our Year 1 target: secure 3 properties in Liberty City for community use. Our Year 5 target: establish a Community Land Trust controlling 14 or more parcels across Liberty City and Overtown. Every target is public. Every dollar is accounted for. Every outcome is measured.

Nil satis nisi optimum. Nothing but the best is good enough. And the best, for Liberty City, is ownership.
