Clean Up to Green Up: Continuing the Overtown Legacy of Community Builders

From Ralph McCartney rebuilding schools to E5 Enclave transforming vacant lots into living infrastructure

ILA
Israel Lee Armstead
President & Chief Visionary
January 20, 2026 · 10 min read

I

n 1966, Ralph McCartney walked into an Overtown school that the county had left to rot. The roof leaked. The floors buckled. The county's position was simple: the school served Black children, and Black children could wait. McCartney didn't wait. He organized volunteers, sourced materials, and rebuilt that school with community labor. No grant. No government program. Just people who refused to accept neglect as their portion.

Sixty years later, E5 Enclave operates in the same tradition — with new tools. Our Clean Up to Green Up program transforms blighted, neglected, and vacant properties into productive community green spaces. The spirit is McCartney's. The technology is ours.

The Problem We Solve

Miami-Dade County has thousands of vacant lots in historically Black neighborhoods. Many are owned by absentee landlords who let them deteriorate into dumping grounds — attracting pests, depressing neighboring property values, and signaling to residents that their neighborhood has been abandoned by the systems that should maintain it.

A vacant lot is not empty — it's full of potential. Every garden we plant is a statement: this land serves this community.

These lots are not just eyesores. They are health hazards. Standing water breeds mosquitoes. Illegal dumping introduces chemicals into the soil and water table. The absence of green canopy in these neighborhoods contributes to urban heat island effects that disproportionately harm elderly residents and children.

Clean Up to Green Up addresses all of these problems simultaneously. We negotiate access agreements with property owners, clean the sites, test the soil, remediate contamination where found, and establish community gardens, pollinator corridors, and gathering spaces. Every project is designed with the community, not for the community.

The Process

Phase one is assessment. We identify candidate lots through community input, county property records, and aerial survey. We prioritize lots in food deserts, near schools, and in areas with the highest concentration of elderly residents — our Homefront Support population.

Phase two is negotiation. Many absentee owners are willing to grant access in exchange for liability protection and maintenance. Some donate the property outright. For those who resist, we work with county code enforcement to ensure minimum maintenance standards are met.

Ralph McCartney rebuilt schools with his bare hands. We rebuild neighborhoods with green infrastructure and digital tools. The spirit is the same.

Phase three is transformation. Community volunteers — our Ambassadors — lead the physical work. Soil testing determines what can be grown safely. Raised beds address contamination where direct ground planting is not advisable. Rain gardens manage stormwater. Native plantings restore habitat. Every element serves multiple purposes.

Phase four is activation. The space is turned over to the community. Regular programming begins — gardening workshops, youth education days, community meals using the harvest. The site becomes a node in the neighborhood's social infrastructure.

Green Infrastructure as Resistance

There is something deeply political about planting a garden on a lot that was supposed to stay empty until a developer was ready for it. A community garden says: this land is in use. This land serves people. This land is not waiting for you.

Every Clean Up to Green Up site is a statement of presence. It is a physical manifestation of the E5 principle that Black communities have the right and the capacity to shape their own environments. Not through permission. Through action.

Ralph McCartney understood this. He didn't petition the county to fix the school. He fixed it. We operate in that tradition. Clean up the lot. Green up the block. Build what the community needs, and let the systems catch up.

Year One Targets

By the end of 2026, Clean Up to Green Up will have transformed a minimum of 6 vacant lots in Overtown and Liberty City. Each site will produce measurable food output, documented through our FarmBlock tracking system. Each site will host at least 4 community programming events. And each site will be maintained by trained community Ambassadors — not outside contractors.

The legacy of Overtown's community builders is not a museum exhibit. It is a living practice. And every lot we transform continues the work that Ralph McCartney and others began — building with our hands, for our people, on our terms.

Dum spiro, spero.
