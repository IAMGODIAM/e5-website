<p class="first">820 NW 64th Street is a real address. The neighborhood around it — Liberty City, Miami — is a real place with a real history, real data, and real people making decisions about what gets built there and who it belongs to. E5 Enclave exists because the people closest to that address decided the answer was theirs to build.</p>

It is a story about structure — what gets built, who owns it, and why those two questions are the same question.

## What the data shows

The Black Distress Index (BDI) is E5 Enclave's sovereign community dataset — eight pillars, 50 cities, every data point sourced and cited under a Creative Commons CC0 license. It measures what the neighborhood already knows and what the policy world keeps rediscovering: structural distress is not randomly distributed. It is precisely located.

Three findings that bear directly on Liberty City:

**Health burden.** Black residents in Miami-Dade's highest-distress census tracts carry a chronic disease burden 2.3 times the county average, concentrated in the cardiovascular and metabolic conditions most directly linked to food environment — the sequelae of food apartheid (USDA Economic Research Service, Food Access Research Atlas, 2023).

**Wealth gap.** Median household wealth in Liberty City's primary census tracts is $1,900, compared to a Miami-Dade county median of $38,400 — a ratio of 1:20 (U.S. Census Bureau, American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates, 2022). Cooperative ownership is not an ideological preference in that context. It is the only ownership model that addresses the gap structurally.

**Food desert density.** The FarmBlock Food Desert Index (FDI) places Liberty City in the top decile for compound food access deficit: distance to full-service grocery, concentration of fast food and liquor retail, and absence of community food infrastructure — all measured simultaneously (USDA ERS, 2023; CoStar commercial real estate data, 2024).

These are not metaphors. They are coordinates.

## The history you have to know to understand the present

Liberty City is not a neighborhood that fell behind. It is a neighborhood that was pushed. The distinction matters for anyone who wants to understand why E5 Enclave is structured the way it is — and why the work starts here.

In the 1940s and 1950s, Miami's Black professional class was concentrated in Overtown — a self-contained economy with its own hotels, theaters, businesses, and civic institutions. What happened to Overtown is well-documented: Interstate 95 was routed directly through it. The decision was not incidental. Urban renewal and highway infrastructure projects displaced 40,000 Black Miami residents between 1956 and 1970, according to the Miami-Dade County Historic Preservation Division. The community that survived migrated north to Liberty City.

Liberty City in the 1970s and 1980s carried the full weight of that displacement — plus the weight of disinvestment, redlining, and the crack cocaine epidemic that the federal government's own investigations later confirmed was seeded by CIA-linked networks (the Gary Webb reporting, the Kerry Committee report). In 1980, Liberty City was the site of the McDuffie uprising — a rebellion that followed the acquittal of four Miami-Dade police officers who beat Arthur McDuffie, a Black insurance salesman, to death following a traffic stop. The rebellion was not irrational. It was a community's refusal to accept that the law would never protect them.

What was rebuilt after 1980 was built on top of that history, not in spite of it. Liberty City became a proving ground for Black cultural production — the birthplace of Miami bass, the incubator of generations of athletes, artists, and institutions. But the structural conditions did not change. The redlining maps changed their name to opportunity zone maps. The food deserts stayed. The wealth gap stayed. The school underfunding stayed.

This is the soil E5 Enclave grows from. Not as a problem to be solved from the outside — as a home to be built from within.

## What FarmBlock is actually building

[FarmBlock](/farmblock/) is E5 Enclave's urban agriculture program. Cooperative farms on vacant land in Liberty City, owned by the community members who tend them, producing food for the neighborhood first and revenue for the cooperative second.

The cooperative ownership model means that every person who works a FarmBlock farm is accruing equity — not wages alone, not volunteer hours, but actual ownership stake in a food-producing asset. When the land appreciates, the cooperative's members appreciate with it. When the harvest generates revenue, the surplus is distributed by cooperative doctrine — back to the members, into the operating fund, into the next site acquisition.

FarmBlock Phase I is in land identification and stakeholder organizing. The [FDI dataset](/record/measure-the-wound/) is the targeting instrument — it locates the highest-need, highest-opportunity parcels by overlaying food access deficit with land availability, ownership status, and transportation access. The methodology is documented and public. The data is free.

The workforce training component runs parallel: structured agricultural training from basic farming literacy through advanced ag-tech, vertical systems, and cooperative business governance. Every graduate is a cooperative owner in the making — competence and equity vesting together. The pipeline is designed so that ownership and competence are inseparable.

This is not a community garden. A community garden is a recreational amenity. FarmBlock is a food system — designed to outlast the grant cycle, the administration, and any individual founder.

## The McCartney Academy and the pipeline it completes

Charles R. Drew K-8 Center sits at 1801 NW 60th Street in Liberty City — four blocks from E5 Enclave's address on 64th Street. It is the school E5's founder walked out of in 2001. It is also the school where Ralph McCartney, community elder and school board figure, spent years as a security guard — watching over the same corridors, the same children, the same zip code.

The McCartney Academy is named in his honor. It is a founding-fund endowment at Charles R. Drew, designed to create a sovereign institution where E5's five pillars — food sovereignty, Black enterprise, reparative economics, cultural sovereignty, and democratic governance — are taught as curriculum. Not as electives. As the core competency framework for students growing up in a community that already embodies all five.

The pipeline from McCartney Academy to FarmBlock to Block to the Boardroom is intentional. A child who learns cooperative economics in middle school is the cooperative farm member of 2035. The cooperative farm member of 2035 is the board director of 2045. The board director of 2045 is the person who makes sure the next generation of Drew students knows their own history.

That is the loop E5 is building. Not a program. Not a grant cycle. A generational loop — self-reinforcing, community-owned, and designed to run without outside permission.

## Why cooperative ownership is the architecture

The instinct of most philanthropic funders when confronted with a community like Liberty City is to deliver services. Food pantries. Job training programs. Financial literacy workshops. The service delivery model is well-intentioned and structurally insufficient. It treats poverty as a condition to be managed rather than a wealth gap to be closed.

The wealth gap in Liberty City is real and measurable. A $36,500 gap between median Black household wealth and Miami-Dade county median is not a behavioral problem. It is not a motivational problem. It is a structural problem that responds to structural solutions.

Cooperative ownership is the structural solution. When a community member owns a stake in a FarmBlock cooperative, three things happen simultaneously that no service delivery model can replicate:

**Asset accumulation.** The ownership stake is a balance sheet entry — real wealth on a real ledger. Land appreciates. A cooperative with 50 member-owners has 50 households building equity instead of zero.

**Cash flow sovereignty.** A cooperative that produces food for its own community generates revenue that stays in the community, distributed by member vote rather than shareholder extraction or foundation discretion.

**Governance competency.** Running a cooperative requires the same skills as running any organization: financial literacy, democratic decision-making, conflict resolution, long-range planning. Every FarmBlock cooperative member is in training for every other leadership role E5 offers — and for every civic and business role in the broader community.

The model scales because it is designed to scale. Each cooperative funds the next. Each graduating cohort mentors the one behind it. The endowment grows. The land base grows. The community owns more of itself each cycle than it did the cycle before.

## What the Coalition is

E5 Enclave is organized around a coalition membership model: 33% neighborhood residents, 17% allied institutions, 50% open to any individual or organization that accepts the mission and meets the intake criteria.

Coalition membership is not a mailing list. It is a governance stake. Members vote on program priorities, elect cohort leaders, and participate in the cooperative ownership structures that FarmBlock, the [McCartney Academy](/programs/mccartney-academy/) founding fund, and the [Block to the Boardroom](/programs/block-to-the-boardroom/) leadership pipeline are built around.

What coalition membership unlocks:

- **Voting rights** in E5 program governance
- **First access** to cooperative ownership opportunities as they come online
- **The Record** — E5 Enclave's institutional publication, where policy work produced by coalition members is published and attributed

Joining is free. The application takes ten minutes. The only qualification is commitment to the mission: sovereign Black community infrastructure, built to last, owned by the people it serves.

## A note on what "sovereignty" actually means

This word gets used a lot in Black political thought, and it deserves a precise definition in the E5 context.

Sovereignty does not mean separation. E5 Enclave pays taxes. It applies for federal grants. It operates in compliance with Florida nonprofit law. Sovereignty, as E5 uses it, means one specific thing: **the community makes its own decisions about its own assets, without requiring outside permission for every choice.**

A food pantry is not sovereign. A cooperative farm with a member-owned title is.

A job training program is not sovereign. A leadership pipeline that places graduates in governance roles within community-owned institutions is.

Sovereignty is not a rhetorical claim. It is a property right. It is a voting right. It is an equity stake. It is the architectural difference between a community that receives services and a community that owns the infrastructure that delivers them.

That is what E5 is building. In Liberty City. At 820 NW 64th Street. Starting now.
